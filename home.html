Over the years, I have been privileged to work with some very talented people.  I hope they didn’t mind that I was watching them very closely and learning how I could emulate their best practices for communications.

Some of my former colleagues were masters at selecting the right vehicles for a particular situation.  They assembled the right mix of power point slides, excel spreadsheets, etc.  They knew when to schedule a meeting as opposed to sending an email or filming a video.  They knew when to make and when to revise a wiki page.

I observed and emulated their tactics.  As a result, I could claim large productivity and knowledge management returns for the departments I supported over the years.

There was one problem.  I wasn’t enlisting enough colleagues who struggled with comms and documentation.  Those folks tended to approach me after a visible knowledge management win and confess that they didn’t believe they could emulate the documentation best practices because of their technical rather than language acumen. 

Or others were skeptical.  They were convinced I must be working nights and weekends in order to keep the wiki in such good shape.  They assumed that I was performing every activity they were (email, IM, meetings) and then also adding on wiki content management as a bonus task.

All of the emotions and opinions expressed made it clear.  The un-enlisted colleagues. . . the ones who were struggling with comms and documentation. . . this domain was very uncomfortable for them.  In order to help share the tactics mastered by a few rock star communicators, I needed to make it safe for them to learn and provide content that they could consume on-demand if they wanted to improve their skills. 

Let's begin with basic questions

1)    Do you believe that changing the information management culture of your organization would help improve productivity?

2)    What if we narrow the scope to your team? 

3)    Or narrow it further to your tasks?  In other words, do you think you would benefit from comms practices I might teach you . . . or that you might observe in a colleague you admire?

4)    Do you think you are capable of making a change in some areas that many consider very sacred and personal – your approach to collaborating with others (meetings, documentation and so on)?  Or do you think these improvements are only possible for people who are already excellent writers?

For those who answered yes to most of the above, you will likely benefit the most from videos above.
